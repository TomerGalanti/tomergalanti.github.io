Research Focus

Building a rigorous science of modern AI

Tomer Galanti is an Assistant Professor in the Department of Computer Science and Engineering at Texas A&M University. His research develops the mathematical foundations of modern AI — from understanding why pre-trained representations generalize and transfer, to building provably capable systems from LLM agents.

Prior to joining Texas A&M, he was a postdoctoral associate at MIT's Center for Brains, Minds & Machines, working with Tomaso Poggio. He received his Ph.D. from Tel Aviv University, advised by Lior Wolf, and interned as a Research Scientist at Google DeepMind in 2021.

A Theory of Directional Neural Collapse in Self-Supervised Learning
Frozen self-supervised representations often transfer with surprisingly few labels, not because they collapse everywhere, but because they collapse in the directions that matter. These two papers show that SSL learns an anisotropic geometry in which within-class variation shrinks sharply along class-separating directions while remaining large in irrelevant subspaces. [1] identifies this mechanism through directional CDNV, showing that contrastive learning implicitly moves toward a supervised clustering geometry and that few-shot error is governed primarily by decision-axis variability rather than total within-class spread. [2] develops this into a full theory of directional neural collapse: once decision-axis variance becomes small, few-shot transfer becomes predictable, and independent downstream tasks are pushed onto nearly orthogonal decision axes, allowing a single pretrained representation to support many tasks with minimal interference. Together, the papers give a geometric explanation for two central facts about SSL: why frozen features work so well in the low-label regime, and why they remain broadly reusable across diverse tasks.

Neural Collapse and Transfer Learning
Why do pre-trained models transfer so well, even with only a handful of examples? In [1], [2], and [3], we give the first theoretical answer through the lens of neural collapse. We show that the geometric structure learned during pretraining is not confined to the source classes, but can extend to unseen target classes in a way that directly enables few-shot generalization. Central to this theory is the Class-Distance Normalized Variance (CDNV), which we introduce as a key predictor of transfer error and which has since become a standard tool in neural collapse research. Together, these works turn neural collapse from an in-distribution training phenomenon into a broader theory of representation transfer, with influence on both the supervised and self-supervised literature.

The Relationship Between Self-Supervised and Supervised Contrastive Learning
In [1] and [2], we show that self-supervised contrastive learning is much closer to supervised learning than previously understood. The first paper proves that the standard contrastive objective closely approximates a supervised surrogate we call negatives-only supervised contrastive learning (NSCL), with a label-agnostic, architecture-independent gap that vanishes as the number of classes grows. The second paper shows that this connection survives far beyond the loss itself: throughout training, CL and NSCL remain strongly aligned in representation space, with provable guarantees for similarity matrices and downstream alignment metrics such as CKA and RSA. Together, these works provide a principled explanation for why contrastive pretraining recovers semantic structure so effectively without labels: it is not merely learning invariances, but implicitly tracking a supervised geometry all along.

SGD and Weight Decay Secretly Minimize Rank
We provide the first general explanation for why neural networks trained with mini-batch SGD often develop low-rank weight matrices. In [3], we show that SGD together with weight decay provably induces an implicit bias toward low-rank solutions across a broad class of modern architectures. In contrast to prior work, our result does not rely on restrictive assumptions about the data, convergence, global optimality, width, or depth. The theory further reveals that this bias becomes stronger with smaller batch sizes, larger learning rates, and stronger weight decay. More broadly, the result suggests that compressibility is not merely an after-effect of training, but a structural property already being shaped by the optimization dynamics themselves.

05
Modularity of Hypernetworks
Hypernetworks offer a radically different way to condition a model: instead of feeding context into a fixed predictor, they generate the predictor’s weights themselves. In [4], we gave one of the first rigorous explanations for why this can be fundamentally more powerful. The paper formalizes modularity as the ability to efficiently realize a different function for each conditioning input, and proves that hypernetworks can achieve this with orders-of-magnitude smaller primary networks than standard embedding-based conditioning methods. Under structured assumptions, the result is even stronger: the total number of trainable parameters needed by a hypernetwork can be orders of magnitude smaller than that of both standard networks and embedding-based alternatives. Conceptually, this provides a mathematical foundation for a central idea behind modern adaptive models: generating weights is not just another implementation trick, but a fundamentally more expressive way to allocate computation across tasks, contexts, and instances.
